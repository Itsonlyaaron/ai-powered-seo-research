AI SEO Principles:


Topical Authority = Topical Coverage × Historical Data. Coverage alone without publishing history yields no authority; history without coverage is also insufficient.

Cost of Retrieval is the most important concept for AI-era SEO: structured, interlinked, machine-readable content gets retrieved and cited more often.

The Algorithmic Trinity — LLM intelligence, search information, and knowledge graph verification — must all be satisfied for AI visibility.

Different LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity) retrieve content differently; content formats and structures must be adapted per platform, not served uniformly.

Semantic content networks, not blog posts, are the atomic unit of modern SEO. A topical map must merge search language with natural language to lower retrieval cost while maximizing entity relevance.

Cannibalization still suppresses rankings — dismissing it is a mistake regardless of what influencer contrarianism suggests.

Brand-driven demand creation amplifies technical SEO because AI engines prefer recognized, historically verifiable entities.



Content Strategy Lessons:


Publish data-backed case studies consistently — they function as both content and proof of concept, compounding brand authority over time.

An "outer section" (informational, trust-signal-heavy content surrounding commercial pages) is not optional — it does the heavy lifting for topical authority and is frequently underbuilt by practitioners.

Content length should be "as long as necessary and as short as possible" — the goal is semantic completeness, not word count inflation.

Internal linking is the infrastructure of topical PageRank: coverage without internal linking reads as a pile of pages, not a topic.

Recency is a mechanism, not just a ranking factor — it keeps attributed claims alive in LLM pipelines and signals ongoing expertise to search engines.

Building community-driven success stories distributes proof of methodology, reducing the persuasion burden on primary content assets.

Actionable Insights:


Audit topical gaps, not just keyword gaps. Use Koray's formula: Historical Data × Topical Coverage. Identify where your site's semantic coverage breaks down — those are your highest-leverage content opportunities.

Reduce cost of retrieval. Serve structured, interlinked, semantically organized content. Consider outputting key content as clean markdown for LLM crawlers.

Build an outer section. Surround commercial pages with comprehensive informational content on adjacent subtopics. This is the trust-signal architecture that enables core topical authority.

Stop using Domain Rating as a topical authority proxy. Audit coverage and publishing history instead.

Map your entity-attribute-value relationships. Before writing any page, define the central entity, its attributes, and the values those attributes carry — this determines semantic depth, not word count.

Adapt content format per LLM. Experiment with serving markdown, structured data, and entity-rich JSON-LD to reduce AI parsing friction.

Fix cannibalization before scaling content. Scaling a topical map without resolving existing cannibalization suppresses the pages you want to rank.

Maintain publishing cadence. Historical data is half the topical authority formula — gaps in publishing history signal unreliability to both search engines and LLMs.

Use AI agents for semantic analysis, not content generation. Koray's 48 GPT agents are designed for NLP analysis, entity extraction, and topical mapping — not for replacing expert editorial judgment.

Don't abandon SEO for AI channels. Google's 87.6% referral traffic share in 2026 makes SEO non-negotiable; AI visibility is additive, not a replacement strategy.
